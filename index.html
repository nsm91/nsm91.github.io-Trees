QGIS Tree Inventory

In the destructive wake of EAB (emerald ash borer), Burr Ridge was faced with removing over a fifth of all parkway trees - exceeding two thousand ash trees. Back logged with removals and new plantings, their tree inventory was facing obsoletion. Not only was I tasked with bringing the inventory up to current working order, but to also create a system that would be easier to update and manage on a casual, day to day basis. As such, I made the inventory compatible with a mobile app.

This map was created specifically for use with the new (and still currently experimental) app QFieldÂ®. It's design is meant to be user friendly even for non-GIS personal and cartographically appealling even on smartphone screen. Below are the methods I employed to acheive these goals.

Accelerated Full Scale Renderings

Goals

Since the innitial view upon opening the app would be the entire village, I wanted this innitial rendering to be as quick as possible. To facilitate this I used maximum geometry simplification for the parcels polygon layer. Additionally, I made both the polyline street layer with actual attribute data and the polygon street layer with curb to curb geometry data invisible at this scale. Likewise I do not have any labels visible at this extent, nor any historic trees that are no longer standing.

Highlights

  • Simplified Parcel Geometries
  • "Invisible" Streets
  • No labels at Full Scale
  • Only Standing Trees Visible

Higher Detail and Information at Magnified Scales

Goals

At zoomed in scales the parcels polygons no longer experience geometry simplifications. Additionally, the street polygon layer is now rendered, allowing for accurate interpretations of right-of-way distances off the curb. Underneath this polygon layer is the still invisible polyline street layer, but yet, the polyline labels do become visible. These labels are the largest and are buffered so as to always stand out. Parcel labels containing street addresses are visible at this scale too, however labels for tree species still are not. Note also that small red dots are now visible, denoting historic locations for trees - important for inventory verification.

Highlights

  • Scale Dependent Renderings
  • "Overlapping" Streets
  • Scale Dependent Labeling
  • Historic Trees Visible

Rule Based Tree Layer

Goals

Since the actual tree data was the focus and driver of this entire project, it is only fitting that they comprise the most robust portion of the map. As such I utilized three levels of rule based options (for styling, rendering, and labeling) to deliver the information in the most immediately useful fashion possible. Only currently standing trees would appear at full zoom, with the conventional symbol, and with a label. All historic trees would only render at a zoomed in scale, as smaller and less pronounced red dots, and would do so without labels, unless user selected, so as to not crowd the map.

Highlights

  • Rule Based Styles
  • Rule Based Renderings
  • Rule Based Labels
