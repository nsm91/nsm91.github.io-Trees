QGIS Tree Inventory

In the destructive wake of EAB (emerald ash borer), Burr Ridge was faced with removing over a fifth of all parkway trees - exceeding two thousand ash trees. Back logged with removals and new plantings, their tree inventory was facing obsoletion. I was tasked with bringing the inventory up to dateas well as creating a system that would be easier to update and manage in the future. As such, I wanted the inventory to be mobile and accessible outside the office.

This inventory was created specifically for use with the new (and still currently experimental) app QFieldÂ® through QGIS. It's design is meant to be user friendly even for non-GIS personal and cartographically appealling even on a smartphone screen. Below is a recounting of some of the goals and their execution.

Accelerated Full Scale Renderings

Goals

Since the innitial view upon opening the app would be the entire village, I wanted this innitial rendering to be as quick as possible. To facilitate this I used maximum geometric simplification for the parcels polygon layer. Only the parcels layer and the trees layer would be innitially visible. Labels are disabled until much larger scales. I chose these two layer because together they gave an adequate sense of the village for identifying a place of interest.

Highlights

  • Simplified Parcel Geometries
  • Minimal Feature Rendering
  • No labels at Full Scale
  • Only Current Trees Visible

Higher Detail and Information at Magnified Scales

Goals

At zoomed in scales the parcels polygons no longer undergo geometric simplification. Additionally, polygons of buildings, road pavement, and driveways now render, giving a less abstract sense of space. This allows for more accurate interpretations of right-of-way distances from the curb. Underneath the street polygon layer is the still invisible street polyline layer, with only their labels are visible. Parcel labels containing street addresses are visible at this scale too.

Highlights

  • Scale Dependent Renderings
  • "Overlapping" Streets
  • Scale Dependent Labeling

Rule Based Tree Layer

Goals

Since the actual tree data was the focus and driver of the entire project, I used three levels of rule based options (for styling, rendering, and labeling) to deliver the information in the most immediately useful fashion possible. Only currently standing trees would appear at full zoom, with the conventional symbol, and then at larger scales with a label. All historic trees would only render at a zoomed in scale, as smaller and less pronounced red dots, and would do so without labels. Trees that need monitoring due to specific structural flaws appear in a different color, as well as trees that are designated for removal.

Highlights

  • Rule Based Styles
  • Rule Based Renderings
  • Rule Based Labels
